This enclosed step stool provides a platform to raise children to the height of a counter top, so they can observe and participate in kitchen activities. The adjustable platform and front step allow the tower to adapt as a child grows.

All parts are made from stock of the same thickness. If you are building this for a standard-height counter (36 inches in the US, or 90 cm. elsewhere), and easy way to acquire the material is to shop in the shelving section of your local home improvement store. They are likely to have hardwood shelving panels 36-by-18 in., or 90-by-45 cm. Three of these will accomodate all parts: one for each side, and one for the platforms and other cross members. A good secondary option is 48-by-24 in. (120-by-60 cm.) "handy panels" of good-quality 3/4 in. (1.9 cm.) plywood.

Making the Ends

{% capture step_size_panels %}{% increment tt_step %}{% endcapture %} {% include step_heading.html number=step_size_panels title="Cut the panels to size" %} {% capture figure_size_panels %}{% increment tt_fig %}{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_size_panels_caption %}Cut two panels to {{tt.height}}cm by {{tt.bottom_depth}}cm.{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_size_panels image="view-panel-size.png" caption=figure_size_panels_caption %}

Start by cutting two panels to {{tt.height}}cm by {{tt.bottom_depth}}cm. Ensure that the {{tt.height}}cm dimension runs with the grain of the wood. When the tower is standing upright, the grain of the wood should run vertically.

If your panels came labeled as this size from the store, check that they are cut square and to exactly matching size.

{% capture step_platform_dados %}{% increment tt_step %}{% endcapture %} {% include step_heading.html number=step_platform_dados title="Cut dados for the platform and step" %} {% capture figure_platform_dados %}{% increment tt_fig %}{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_platform_dados_caption %}Cut dados {{tt.recess_depth}}cm deep for the platform and front step to slide into.{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_platform_dados image="view-platform_dados.png" caption=figure_platform_dados_caption %}

The most important cuts in this build are the dados into which the platform and step fit. If the dados in the left side don't align with the dados in the right, the tower won't stand straight, or the platform may not even fit. So, make those cuts while the stock still has plenty of square side from which to reference.

The dados should be at least as wide as the stock you're using ({{tt.thickness}}cm). If you cut them just a half millimeter or so wider ({{tt.thickness | plus: 0.05}}cm), the platform will slide in and out more easily.

The dados should be {{tt.recess_depth}}cm deep to give a good ledge to support the platform and step, without creating a weak spot in the side.

The platform slides in from the cabinet side of the tower. Make sure to cut these {{tt.platform_depth}}cm long dados from the right side of one panel, and from the left side of the other. The top edge of the dados should be at {% for h in platform_heights %}{% if h == platform_heights.last %}and {{tt.h}}cm{% else %}{{tt.h}}cm, {% endif %}{% endfor %} from the bottom edge.

The step slides into its {{tt.front_step_depth}}cm dado from the opposite side of each panel. These dados should be at {% for h in front_step_heights %}{% if h == front_step_heights.last %}and {{tt.h}}cm{% else %}{{tt.h}}cm{% endif %}{% endfor %} from the bottom edge.

If you accidentally cut all platform dados from the same side of each panel, or you don't want to deal with squaring off the inner end of the dado, it's completely fine to continue cutting all the way through to the other side. A stopped dado, as designed, provides a nice positive feedback when inserting the platform, but it's not necessary for the tower to function.

{% capture step_support_rabbets %}{% increment tt_step %}{% endcapture %} {% include step_heading.html number=step_support_rabbets title="Cut rabbets and grooves for the cross-member supports" %} {% capture figure_support_rabbets %}{% increment tt_fig %}{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_support_rabbets_caption %}Cut rabbets and grooves {{tt.recess_depth}}cm deep for the cross-member supports to fit into.{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_support_rabbets image="view-support_rabbets.png" caption=figure_support_rabbets_caption %}

Next cut rabbets and grooves for the permanently-fixed support members. All rabbets and grooves are {{tt.recess_depth}}cm deep and {{tt.thickness}}cm wide, like the platform and step dados. But, since these pieces don't need to slide, keepthing the width and length of these recesses to the exact dimensions of the pieces that will fit in them, is a good idea.

Along the cabinet edge, cut a rabbet {{tt.narrow_support_height}}cm long, starting at the top edge. Also cut a rabbet {{tt.narrow_support_height}}cm long, starting {{tt.thickness}}cm up from the bottom edge. Finally, cut a rabbet {{tt.wide_support_height}}cm long starting {{tt.wide_support_height | times: 1.25}}cm from the top edge.

Cut a groove from the bottom edge through the lower wall of the lowest front step dado, exactly in the middle of that dado. Also cut a groove {{tt.safety_rail_height}}cm long starting at the top edge, with the edge farthest from the cabinet side being {{tt.platform_depth}}cm from the cabinet side.

{% capture step_front_angle %}{% increment tt_step %}{% endcapture %} {% include step_heading.html number=step_front_angle title="Cut the front at a ladder angle." %} {% capture figure_front_angle %}{% increment tt_fig %}{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_front_angle image="view-front_angle.png" caption="Cut the front from the lowest step to the support rail." %}

Draw a line from the top outer corner of the lowest step dado, to the top outer corner of the safety rail. Cut along this line, and discard the triangle offcut.

{% capture step_handholds %}{% increment tt_step %}{% endcapture %} {% include step_heading.html number=step_handholds title="Cut out holes for handholds." %} {% capture figure_handholds %}{% increment tt_fig %}{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_handholds_circles %}{{figure_handholds}}a{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_handholds_circles_caption %}Start the handholds by drilling holes {{tt.handhold_size[1]}}cm in diameter at either end of each handhold.{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_handholds_circles image="view-handholds_circles.png" caption=figure_handholds_circles_caption %}

Start the handholds by marking points {{tt.handhold_size[1]}}cm from the angled edge cut in {% include step_link.html number=step_front_angle %}, and {% for h in handhold_heights %}{% if h == handhold_heights.last %}and {{tt.h}}cm{% else %}{{tt.h}}cm, {% endif %}{% endfor %} up from the bottom edge. Next, marking another point, also {{tt.handhold_size[1]}}cm from the angle edge, and {{tt.handhold_size[0]}}cm below each of the first points.

If you have a hole saw, drill a {{tt.handhold_size[1]}}cm hole at each point you just marked. If you don't have a hole saw, use a compass to draw a {{tt.handhold_size[1] | divided_by: 2}}cm radius circle at each point, then drill the larges hole you can with whatever bit you have smaller than that circle at the center point.

Repeat this process for one more handhold along the top edge. Place the first point at {{tt.top_handhold_offset}}cm from the cabinet-side edge, {{tt.handhold_size[1]}}cm down from the top edge. Place the second point an additional {{tt.handhold_size[0]}}cm from the front edge.

{% capture figure_handholds_flats %}{{figure_handholds}}b{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_handholds_flats image="view-handholds_flats.png" caption="Complete the handholds by sawing out the waste between the circles." %}

To finish the handholds, lay a straightedge against the edges of each pair of circles, and draw a line from one to the other. Saw long these lines and remove the waste. You may need to use a rasp or sandpaper to even the last bit of material where the flat meets the edge of the circle.

To make the handholds more comfortable, round or chamfer the edges. If you have a router with a roundover bit or chamfering bit, this should be quick work. If you don't, a rasp or coarse sandpaper will be effective.

{% capture step_windows %}{% increment tt_step %}{% endcapture %} {% include step_heading.html number=step_windows title="Cut windows (optional)." %} {% capture figure_windows %}{% increment tt_fig %}{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_windows_circles %}{{figure_windows}}a{% endcapture %} {% capture figure_windows_circles_caption %}Start the windows by drilling holes {{tt.handhold_size[1]}}cm in diameter at the corners of each window.{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_windows_circles image="view-windows_circles.png" caption=figure_windows_circles_caption %}

The windows in the top and bottom of the unit are optional. The top one removes a bit of weight from the unit, and helps to lower the center of gravity. The bottom one also removes weight, and makes it less likely that unevenness in the floor will cause the tower to rock.

The process for making the windows is similar to the process for making the handholds. Start the upper window by making a mark {{tt.upper_window_inset}}cm down from the top edge, and in the same distance from the cabinet edge. Make a second mark also {{tt.upper_window_inset}}cm down from the top edge, but {{tt.upper_window_top_depth}}cm farther from the cabinet edge ({{tt.upper_window_inset | plus: tt.upper_window_top_depth}}cm from the cabinet edge in total). Make a third mark {{tt.upper_window_inset}}cm in from the cabinet edge, but {{tt.upper_window_height}}cm below the first mark ({{tt.upper_window_height | plus: tt.upper_window_inset}}cm total from the top edge). Make your final mark the same distance from the top edge, but {{tt.upper_window_bottom_depth}}cm farther from the cabinet edge ({{tt.upper_window_bottom_depth | plus: upper_window_bottom_depth}}cm total). Drill holes at each mark.

Start the bottom window by making a mark {{tt.lower_window_height}}cm from the bottom edge, and {{tt.lower_window_upper_corner_offset | plus: tt.lower_window_inset}}cm from the angled edge at that height. Mark a second point at the same distance from the bottom edge, and {{tt.front_step_depth}}cm farther in from the front angled edge. Drill at each mark. (The lower corners of this window are not rounded, so they don't need drilling.)

{% capture figure_windows_flats %}{{figure_windows}}b{% endcapture %} {% include figure.html number=figure_windows_flats image="view-window_flats.png" caption="Cut between the edges of the drilled holes to remove the windows" %}

Using a straightedge, draw lines between the top edges of the top circles, the bottom edges of the bottom cricle, the right edges of the right circles, and the left edges of the left circles, of the top window. Saw along these lines, and discard the material that comes out of the center.

Make a mark on the bottom edge of the panel {{tt.lower_window_inset}}cm from the non-cabinet corner. Make a second mark {{tt.lower_winddow_inset | plus: lower_window_bottom_depth}}cm from the same corner. using a straightedge, connect the first point to the right side of the right circle above it, connect the second point to the left side of the left circle, and finally connect the top edges of the circles. Saw along these lines, and discard the trapezoid that comes off.

Round or chamfer the edges of these windows, like you did the handholds. Also round or chamfer the outer edges of each panel. The side panels are now complete.
